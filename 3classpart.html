Class Participation: The Game

University classes often award patronising and conciliatory marks to students for the very act of participating in class. Better known as participation marks, this blatant carrot-on-stick mechanism inspired me to create Class Participation: The Game for partial completement of the class NM2207 Computational Media Literacy, hosted by the Department of Communications and New Media at the National University of Singapore, which introduced students to HTML, CSS and Javascript, and the raphael.js library.

Play Game

Singaporean students are crudely quiet. It's in our culture to mind our own business (unless you're an aunty, maybe), and this introverted quality remains true within the walls of school classrooms. This is largely the reason why our local universities openly entice students to emit sounds using their mouths with the one thing they all obviously care about: grades. While patronising at best, this tactic has become commonplace in the grading schemes of almost all university classes, with students, unsurprisingly, saying nothing about being the ass in this carrot-on-stick scheme.

Enter Class Participation: The Game, a web browser mini-game powered almost exclusively using the Raphaël Javascript library. In it, the player acts as a typical student, whose task is to wave their eager arm in the air (click and drag, preferably using a mouse) with honest zeal in order to catch the attention of their beloved instructor. Once they have done so, they are given 5 seconds to make their priceless contribution to academia. The player types and submits literally anything and is awarded one point for every character they type. This continues until the 30-second class is over.

You can play the game by clicking on the "Play Game" button at the end of this paragraph. Ensure your browser is in full screen before loading the game, or refresh your browser after it has entered full screen. Please note that this game was not created for mobile browsers, and while it loads on mobile, its mechanics are only operational on desktop browsers.
